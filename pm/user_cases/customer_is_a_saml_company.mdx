# Use case: a B2B SaaS customer is a whole company (domain-gated tenant)

## Summary

A web app that we own sits behind **OpenAuthFederated**, but unlike the
[internal-admin use case](./admin_by_our_own_company.mdx) this web app is a **multi-tenant B2B
SaaS product**. Its users are not our employees — they are the employees of **our customers**.
A customer often signs up and **pays for their entire company**, and they want onboarding to be
effortless for their people. Two onboarding shapes must be supported:

* **Domain-based admission (the headline case):** the customer says "our company is
  `acme.com` — anybody with a verified `@acme.com` identity should be admitted into *our* tenant."
  No per-user invitations; the domain is the membership rule.
* **Explicit values (allowlist):** the customer instead supplies specific emails (or a small set
  of patterns) that should be admitted — useful for a pilot, a small team, or contractors.

A customer that runs its own identity provider is a **"SAML company"**: they bring their own
**SAML 2.0 / OIDC** IdP, and we federate the customer's tenant to it so their employees sign in
with their existing corporate credentials. Domain admission and customer-owned SSO compose: the
verified domain both routes the login to the right tenant *and* enforces who is allowed in.

Each customer maps to one **organization (tenant)** in OpenAuthFederated. The web app calls
OpenAuthFederated to (1) create the tenant, (2) claim and verify the customer's domain, (3) admit
qualifying users (JIT) into that tenant, optionally via the customer's own IdP, and — critically
— (4) **answer the runtime question** the web app asks on every tenant-scoped request: *is this
signed-in user authenticated, and do they qualify for this particular tenant?* OpenAuthFederated
performs that security check and returns the verdict (via the session token's org/permission
claims plus, when needed, a stateful membership/verify call).

> **Naming rule.** Internal spec for OpenAuthFederated, our open-source, self-hosted identity
> platform. Never name the commercial hosted provider we model after; describe capabilities
> generically.

## Actors

| Actor | Role in this use case |
| --- | --- |
| **Customer company** | The buyer. Owns a domain (e.g. `acme.com`) and possibly its own SAML/OIDC IdP. Wants its whole company admitted into its tenant. |
| **Customer employee** | An end user signing in to the SaaS app; must resolve to the customer's tenant and qualify for it. |
| **Consuming web app (our SaaS)** | Multi-tenant product. Onboards customers, calls OpenAuthFederated to create tenants / verify domains / admit users, and asks OpenAuthFederated "does this user qualify for this tenant?" on every tenant-scoped request. |
| **OpenAuthFederated** | The identity/auth layer. Models tenants as organizations, enforces verified domains, federates to the customer's IdP, JIT-provisions members, and answers authorization questions. |
| **Customer IdP** | (SAML company case) The customer's Google Workspace / Okta-style IdP that authenticates their employees. |

## Why we do it this way

* **Self-serve company onboarding.** The customer proves they own `acme.com` (DNS TXT) once;
  thereafter their employees flow in automatically. We charge per company, not per manually
  invited seat.
* **The tenant boundary is authoritative and ours.** Each customer is an isolated
  [organization](#tenants-are-organizations); domain → connection → org routing is enforced
  server-side, so a user from `acme.com` can never land in `globex.com`'s tenant.
* **Customers keep their own login.** A "SAML company" federates its own IdP; we never hold their
  employees' passwords, and offboarding at the customer's IdP propagates to us.
* **One runtime contract.** Whatever the admission shape, the web app asks the same question at
  runtime — verify the token, confirm tenant membership, check permissions — and OpenAuthFederated
  answers it.

## Tenants are organizations

A customer = an **Organization** (tenant) in OpenAuthFederated. The org is the unit of
multi-tenancy and owns its SSO connection(s), verified domains, SCIM config, roles, members, and
settings. Documented in:

* `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/organizations.mdx` (control plane: create tenant, attach identity config, settings such as `enforce_domain`, `allow_jit_provisioning`, `default_role_id`).
* `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/backend/organizations.mdx` (runtime: `allowed_domains`, memberships, resolved permissions, `max_allowed_memberships` seat cap).

The Organization object carries `allowed_domains` — "verified domains whose users auto-join this
org (domain enforcement)" — which is precisely the "anybody on our domain gets into our tenant"
rule the customer is asking for.

## Two admission shapes

### 1. Domain-based admission (the customer specifies a domain)

The customer's domain is claimed by their org and **verified by DNS TXT**, then enforced. Any
active, verified identity on that domain auto-joins the tenant via **Just-In-Time provisioning**
on first sign-in — no per-user invite. Documented at
`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/domains.mdx`.

* Claim: `POST /v1/domains` with `organization_id` + `domain`.
* Verify: `POST /v1/domains/{dmn_id}/verify` after the customer publishes the TXT record.
* A domain can be claimed by **one org only** (a second claim returns `409`), which guarantees
  tenant isolation by domain.
* The org's `settings.enforce_domain` + `allow_jit_provisioning` (and `allowed_domains` on the
  Backend org object) implement "everyone on this domain, automatically."

### 2. Explicit values (allowlist / specific emails)

For a pilot or a partial rollout the customer supplies explicit identifiers instead of (or in
addition to) a domain. Documented at
`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/backend/invitations.mdx`:

* **Allowlist identifiers** — `POST /v1/allowlist_identifiers` with a full email
  (`alice@acme.com`) or a pattern (`*@acme.com`). Admits matching identities as explicit
  exceptions, even outside the verified domain (e.g. a contractor at `partner.com`).
* **Invitations** — `POST /v1/invitations` with `email_address`, `organization_id`, and `role`
  to proactively place a named person into the tenant with a role on acceptance.
* **Blocklist identifiers** — `POST /v1/blocklist_identifiers` to deny a specific address.

The sign-in evaluation order (blocklist → domain enforcement → allowlist → pending invitation →
otherwise reject) is specified in `invitations.mdx`, "How identifiers interact with domain
enforcement." This is exactly how "a domain **or** sample/explicit values" coexist.

### Optional overlay: the customer is a SAML company (brings its own IdP)

When the customer runs its own IdP, create a per-tenant **SSO connection** federating that org to
the customer's SAML/OIDC IdP — `POST /v1/sso_connections` with `organization_id`, then exchange
metadata (`PATCH .../{conn_id}`). The customer's verified domain routes their employees' logins to
*their* connection. Documented at
`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/sso-connections.mdx`. The
customer's IdP groups can be mapped to tenant roles via `group_mapping`
(`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/rbac.mdx`), and lifecycle can
be kept in sync with **SCIM 2.0** (`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/scim.mdx`).

## End-to-end flow

### A. Customer onboarding (driven by our SaaS app's admin UI / backend)

When a customer signs up for a company plan, our SaaS backend calls the **Platform/Backend API**
(secret key, `https://api.<your-domain>/v1`):

1. **Create the tenant** — `POST /v1/organizations` with the customer's name and `allowed_domains`
   (or set them after verification). Optionally `max_allowed_memberships` for seat caps and
   `default_role_id` for the role JIT users get.
   Ref: `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/organizations.mdx`.
2. **Claim + verify the customer's domain** — `POST /v1/domains`, show the customer the DNS TXT
   record, then `POST /v1/domains/{dmn_id}/verify`. Surface progress in our onboarding UI; listen
   for the `domain.verified` webhook.
   Ref: `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/domains.mdx`.
3. **(SAML company) Create + activate the tenant's SSO connection** — `POST /v1/sso_connections`,
   exchange IdP metadata, optionally set `group_mapping` and enable SCIM.
   Refs: `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/sso-connections.mdx`,
   `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/scim.mdx`.
4. **(Allowlist case) Add explicit identifiers / invitations** — `POST /v1/allowlist_identifiers`
   and/or `POST /v1/invitations`.
   Ref: `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/backend/invitations.mdx`.
5. **Define tenant roles** and a default role for JIT members — `POST /v1/roles`, set org
   `default_role_id`.
   Ref: `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/rbac.mdx`.

The onboarding pages in our SaaS app may be assembled from drop-in components — `<CreateOrganization>`,
`<OrganizationProfile>` (members/invitations/roles/settings) — documented at
`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/sdk/components/organization.mdx`.

### B. Employee sign-in (through the UI)

1. Employee opens our SaaS app and starts sign-in. The **Frontend API**
   (`https://auth.<your-domain>/v1`,
   `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/frontend/overview.mdx`) creates a
   `SignIn` and redirects to the appropriate IdP — the customer's own IdP (SAML company) or the
   default federation.
2. The IdP authenticates the human; OpenAuthFederated validates the assertion/token and applies
   **domain enforcement**: the email's verified domain (`hd` + `email_verified` on OIDC) must match
   a `verified` domain, which routes the identity to the owning tenant. Out-of-domain / unverified
   identities are rejected with `domain_not_allowed`
   (`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/domains.mdx`).
3. The user is **JIT-provisioned** into that tenant (or matched if SCIM pre-provisioned), assigned
   the default/mapped role, and a **session** is established with a short-lived JWT carrying the
   org context and permissions
   (`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/backend/sessions.mdx`).
4. For users who belong to more than one tenant, our app presents `<OrganizationSwitcher>` /
   `<OrganizationList>` to pick the active org; switching sets `orgId` and re-scopes permissions
   (`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/sdk/components/organization.mdx`).

### C. Runtime authorization — "does this user qualify for this tenant?"

This is the core question the SaaS app asks OpenAuthFederated on every tenant-scoped request. The
answer comes from the token's claims, backed by a stateful check when certainty is required:

* **Read the org + permissions from the token.** The session JWT carries `org`/`org_id`, `roles`,
  and `permissions`. The app confirms the token's org matches the tenant being accessed and that
  the required `<feature>:<action>` permission is present.
  * Front end: `useAuth().has({ permission })`, `<Protect>`, `useOrganization()`
    (`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/getting_started/read-the-user.mdx`,
    `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/sdk/components/control.mdx`).
  * Middleware: match the tenant route and `auth.protect((has) => has({ permission }))`
    (`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/getting_started/protect-routes.mdx`).
* **Backend (authoritative) verification.** The SaaS backend verifies the token and confirms tenant
  membership:

  ```ts
  import { authClient } from '@auth/backend'

  // 1. Verify the short-lived JWT (networkless).
  const claims = await authClient.verifyToken(bearerToken)

  // 2. Confirm the token is scoped to the tenant being accessed.
  if (claims.org_id !== requestedOrgId) throw new ForbiddenException('wrong tenant')

  // 3. Confirm the user still has a membership in that tenant (authoritative).
  const { data: members } = await authClient.organizations.getOrganizationMembershipList({
    organizationId: requestedOrgId,
  })
  const member = members.find(m => m.user_id === claims.sub)
  if (!member) throw new ForbiddenException('not a member of this tenant')

  // 4. Check the required permission.
  if (!member.permissions.includes('jfk:read')) throw new ForbiddenException()
  ```

  Refs: `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/sdk/backend-sdk.mdx`,
  `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/backend/organizations.mdx`.
* **Immediate revocation for sensitive actions.** Call `POST /v1/sessions/{session_id}/verify`
  (`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/backend/sessions.mdx`) so a user
  offboarded at the customer's IdP (SCIM deprovision → session revoke) cannot act on a
  not-yet-expired token.

### D. Offboarding / deprovision

When the customer removes an employee in their own IdP, **SCIM** deactivates the user in the
tenant, **force-revokes their sessions**, and emits `user.deleted` / `session.revoked` webhooks;
short token TTLs finish the job. Documented at
`/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/scim.mdx` ("Deprovisioning and
revocation") and `/Users/bryan/BGit/Bryan_git/OpenAuthFederated/docs/apis/platform/webhooks.mdx`.

## Security

* **Tenant isolation by verified domain.** A domain is claimed by exactly one org; domain
  enforcement runs server-side after IdP validation and before a session is issued, so identities
  can only enter their own tenant. (`/docs/apis/platform/domains.mdx`.)
* **Domain ownership is proven, not asserted.** A customer cannot claim `acme.com` without
  publishing the DNS TXT challenge; an unverified domain is never used for sign-in.
* **No customer passwords held** when the customer federates its own IdP; we are the SP side only.
  (`/docs/apis/platform/sso-connections.mdx`.)
* **Authoritative, server-side tenant check.** The web app must verify `claims.org_id` matches the
  tenant **and** confirm a live membership; front-end/token checks alone are not the boundary.
* **Immediate revocation** via the hybrid session model + SCIM deprovision + short TTLs.
* **Seat caps and abuse limits** via `max_allowed_memberships`; blocklist identifiers for known-bad
  addresses. (`/docs/apis/backend/organizations.mdx`, `/docs/apis/backend/invitations.mdx`.)
* **Strict CORS + public-safe keys** on the browser surface; secret keys stay server-side.
  (`/docs/apis/frontend/overview.mdx`.)
* **Full audit trail** per tenant: `user.sign_in`, `user.sign_in.rejected` (out-of-domain),
  `user.provisioned`, `user.deprovisioned`, `session.revoked`, plus all config changes, filterable
  by `organization_id`. (`/docs/apis/platform/audit-logs.mdx`.)

## Acceptance criteria

1. A customer can self-serve onboard: create a tenant, claim `acme.com`, publish a DNS TXT record,
   and verify it; `domain.verified` fires and the tenant becomes live.
2. With the domain verified + enforced, any active `@acme.com` employee signing in is
   JIT-provisioned into the `acme.com` tenant with the default role — **no per-user invite**.
3. An identity outside the tenant's verified domain (and not on its allowlist / without a pending
   invitation) is **rejected** with `domain_not_allowed`, recorded as `user.sign_in.rejected`.
4. The explicit-values path works: a customer-supplied `*@acme.com` allowlist entry or an
   `alice@acme.com` invitation admits the right people, and a blocklist entry denies a specific
   address.
5. A "SAML company" customer's employees sign in through the **customer's own IdP**, land in the
   correct tenant, and receive roles mapped from the customer's IdP groups.
6. On every tenant-scoped request the web app can ask OpenAuthFederated "is this user authenticated
   and a qualifying member of *this* tenant?" and get a correct verdict: token verified,
   `org_id` matches the tenant, live membership confirmed, permission present.
7. A user accessing a tenant they do not belong to is denied (wrong-tenant `403`), even with a
   valid token for another tenant.
8. Offboarding at the customer IdP (SCIM deprovision) revokes the tenant member's sessions
   promptly; `user.deleted` + `session.revoked` are emitted and audited under that
   `organization_id`.
