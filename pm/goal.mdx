# OpenAuthFederated — Goal

**OpenAuthFederated is our open-source authentication and identity platform.** It is a
self-hosted, open-source equivalent of the hosted commercial identity providers (Auth0, AWS
Cognito, and similar SaaS identity services). The goal is to give ourselves — and anyone who
adopts the project — a complete identity layer that gates access to applications, without
depending on, paying for, or being locked into a proprietary vendor.

## Why we are building it

We want the full value of a hosted identity provider — federated sign-in, provisioning,
role-based authorization, sessions, and the security around them — but delivered as software
**we own and control**:

* **Open source.** The entire platform is open and inspectable. There are no closed components,
  no per-seat billing, and no usage metering. Anyone can run it, audit it, and extend it.
* **Self-hosted.** It runs on our own infrastructure, under our own keys and policies. Identity
  data and authorization decisions stay in our hands.
* **No vendor lock-in.** Upstream identity providers supply groups and roles as *inputs*, but the
  authoritative authorization model lives in our own backend and database. We can change upstream
  IdPs without rebuilding our application's permission model.
* **A drop-in identity layer.** An application can sit behind OpenAuthFederated for *all* of its
  authentication and authorization, instead of rebuilding auth screens, session handling, and
  RBAC from scratch.

## What the platform delivers

OpenAuthFederated provides a complete, self-hostable identity layer:

* **Authentication methods** — federated SSO via **SAML 2.0** and **OIDC**, social logins, and
  enterprise IdP connections.
* **Identity federation** — acts as the Service-Provider-side identity/auth layer between an app
  and an upstream Identity Provider; the app trusts the platform's asserted identity and never
  handles the user's upstream password.
* **Domain enforcement** — restrict sign-in to one or more verified company domains; reject any
  identity outside the allowed domain(s), including unverified emails and out-of-org accounts.
* **Provisioning** — **Just-In-Time (JIT)** user creation on first valid sign-in, plus **SCIM
  2.0** directory sync for full lifecycle management (provision / update / deprovision) with
  webhooks.
* **Organizations / multi-tenancy** — model orgs and tenants so the platform can gate more than
  one app or customer.
* **RBAC** — map upstream groups to roles, and roles to `<feature>:<action>` permissions, with the
  authoritative model kept in our own backend.
* **Hybrid session model** — short-lived **JWT access tokens** refreshed by a longer-lived,
  rotating, **HttpOnly** cookie, backed by server-side session records that allow **immediate
  revocation**. Stateless verification plus stateful revocation.
* **Security** — MFA inherited from the upstream IdP (with optional step-up reverification), HTTPS
  only, `SameSite` + `HttpOnly` cookies, CSP headers, no long-lived secrets in `localStorage`, and
  full auditing of sign-in / sign-out / refresh / permission-denied / admin actions.
* **No password surface** — when federating to an upstream IdP, the platform stores **no end-user
  passwords**, eliminating that entire class of attack.
* **UI components** — drop-in sign-in / sign-up / account-management UI so apps don't rebuild auth
  screens.

## First real use

The first consumer of OpenAuthFederated is an internal company web app gated to our company
**Google Workspace (G Suite)** on the `whitehatengineering.com` domain. Google Workspace is the
single source of truth for who is an employee: a person must have an active Google Workspace
account on the allowed domain to log in — no app-specific passwords, no other social logins, no
anonymous access. This reference deployment is the canonical example of how the platform is used,
and it proves out the federation, domain enforcement, provisioning, RBAC, and session model the
project is built to deliver.

## The bottom line

The goal of OpenAuthFederated is to **own our identity layer outright** — an open-source,
self-hosted platform that does everything a hosted commercial identity provider does, while
keeping the code, the data, and the authorization model entirely under our control.
